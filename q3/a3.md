**1. What makes a race car infotainment system different from a normal mobile app?**
A 'normal mobile app' (in general) does not need to be as stringent about certain factors that affect the user experience as it isn't as critical to the operation of something or the safety of someone as the infotainment system of a race car.

An infotainment system relays information to the driver about the vehicle. The importance of this information can be necessary in making a decision that can have vital consequences. The speed, temperature of the battery, and other conditions of the vehicle are all things that of course need to be reported accurately and with minimal latency to ensure the safety of those involved. Reporting things accurately and promptly, informing the driver effectively with minimal cognitive load are all contributing factors to this

These things are still desired in normal mobile apps, but at times it is ignored, with other things being prioritized like user engagement or monetization (e.g. YouTube videos can still display comments incorrectly but shopping integrations/premium plans continue to undergo several additions/changes/fixes).

Environmental constraints and errors are a unique aspect of infotainment systems as, unlike most mobile apps, they do not need to deal with messy real-world data, or necessarily need to do computation onboard a moving vehicle.

**2. Identify and explain _four_ key principles of good infotainment system design.**

1. Fail-safe behavior (very related to Robustness to dropped or delayed telemetry / Predictability / Handling corrupted or partial data / Smooth degradation under failure) - ensuring that in the event of failure or other malfunction that a safe state is preserved. Important to further limit any undefined or unforeseen behavior, and to keep the result controlled, e.g. if the battery temperature gauge fails not following this principle would involve not alerting the driver or the interface potentially crashing, putting safety at risk.

2. Prioritisation of critical information (very related to Clear and consistent UI states / Minimal driver distraction) - showing more vital information over less important information. Not doing this would involve prioritising something like current battery capacity over battery temperature or speed, causing inconvenience from a readability standpoint and in the worst case again degrading safety.

3. Maintainability/modularity - How easy it is to add/remove parts of the infotainment system, both in terms of maintainability and driver experience. Not following this would look like having a very interdependent codebase, where, in the event of a new feature being added or a change to the system being requested, would be more difficult than necessary to implement. Also the driver may not be able to interact with the system as easily (e.g. not being able to monitor the battery stats more closely for a test due to a design that isn't modular)

4. Reliability - perhaps most important of all for infotainment software, reliably following the concepts above. Without reliable software, unnecessary risk can occur. Rigorous testing and listening for feedback are essential.

**3. Describe how you would test an infotainment interface to ensure it is safe and reliable.**
One would do this by:
- Stress-testing with rapid incoming telemetry
- Simulating network dropouts or corrupted messages
- Simulating extreme temperature and vibration
- Checking for UI freezes, rendering spikes, or memory leaks
- Testing driver readability and response time
- Logging and fault detection
